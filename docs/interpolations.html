Appendix - one-dimensional interpolations¶

As being discussed before, one can use an arbitrary scheme to interpolate the scalar potential \(\psi\). By default, cubic and spectral interpolations are adopted in \(x\) and \(y\) directions, respectively and used in src/kernel/interpolate_scalar_potential.c. Here they are briefly reviewed for completeness.

Description¶

This function interpolates a periodic signal to a new (refined or coarsened) coordinate. As a first step, the original discrete signal \(y_i\), \(i = 0, 1, \cdots, n1-1\), is projected to a spectral space using Fourier transform:

\[Y_j = \mathcal{F} \left[ y_i \right],\]

where \(Y_j\) contains power spectra of the original dataset, which is extended by adding zeros to the higher frequency part.

For instance, we consider the following signal (n1 = 8):

\[\begin{bmatrix} Y_0 & Y_1 & Y_2 & Y_3 & \frac{Y_4+Y_4^*}{2} & Y_3^* & Y_2^* & Y_1^*, \end{bmatrix}\]

where asterisk denotes complex conjugate of the quantity. We would like to interpolate this dataset to a refined coordinate (say n2 = 12), which is achieved by

\[\begin{bmatrix} Y_0 & Y_1 & Y_2 & Y_3 & \frac{Y_4}{2} & 0 & 0 & 0 & \frac{Y_4^*}{2} & Y_3^* & Y_2^* & Y_1^*, \end{bmatrix}\]

where higher frequency part between \(\frac{Y_4}{2}\) and \(\frac{Y_4^*}{2}\) are padded by zero.

Note that, because of the redundancy, only half of the spectral data needs to be kept, i.e.,

\[\begin{bmatrix} Y_0 & Y_1 & Y_2 & Y_3 & \frac{Y_4+Y_4^*}{2} \end{bmatrix}\]

(whose length is \(n_1/2+1\)) and

\[\begin{bmatrix} Y_0 & Y_1 & Y_2 & Y_3 & \frac{Y_4}{2} & 0 & 0 \end{bmatrix}\]

(whose length is \(n_2/2+1\)) are sufficient to describe the original data in physical space.

Warning

This function only accepts bounded signals, i.e., the first points of x1s and x2s should be matched (x1s[0] = x2s[0]). Same holds for the last points (x1s[-1] = x2s[-1]).

Description¶

This function interpolates a bounded signal to a new (refined or coarsened) locations by fitting piecewise third-order polynomials \(y = a_3 x^3 + a_2 x^2 + a_1 x^1 + a_0 x^0\). Since a third-order polynomials have four degrees of freedom, four points are needed to determine all coefficients. Thus, in the vicinity of the walls, quadratic functions are used instead.
